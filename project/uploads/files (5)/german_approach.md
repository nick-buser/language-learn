# German — Approach & Roadmap

## Aim
Reach a level where you can read Dark / Babylon Berlin scripts and German genre TV without subs, eventually move into Kafka, Sebald, Mann, and German-language philosophy in the original.

## Current Level (Self-Reported)
- A2 / B1, several years lapsed
- Lessons earlier, mostly self-taught after
- Likely-strong: basic grammar (cases, articles, verb conjugation)
- Likely-weak: vocab depth, complex syntax processing, listening at speed, modal particles

## The Specific Bottleneck
German is unusual among major languages in that the grammar is genuinely harder than the cultural integration. You've done the hard part — case system, verb-second, separable verbs, gender. What remains is vocab depth, dealing with the literary/philosophical register's compound nouns and embedded clauses, and refreshing what's atrophied.

This is one of the best ROI cases of all your languages, because you're much closer to payoff than your time-since-study suggests. A lapsed B1 generally comes back to active B1 in 6–10 weeks.

## What You Already Have
- Whatever grammar you internalized at A2/B1 will come back faster than expected
- English overlap means German vocabulary acquisition is more compounding than for Korean or Mandarin
- Latinate/Greek loanwords work nearly identically across English and German

## High-Payoff Early Focus

1. **Modal particles** (Modalpartikeln): doch, mal, eben, halt, ja, schon, wohl, denn. These are the single biggest barrier between textbook German and real spoken/written German. They carry meaning that English handles with intonation. "Komm doch mal her" is not "Come here" — it's something closer to "Come here, won't you" with an implied "I've been waiting on you." Most courses defer these to B2+; push them forward to B1.

2. **Compound noun decoding strategy.** Treat compounds as parsing problems: read right-to-left, the rightmost noun is the head, prior elements modify it. *Vergangenheitsbewältigung* = Bewältigung (mastering) ← Vergangenheit (the past). Once you have a strategy, hundred-letter words become manageable rather than intimidating.

3. **Subjunctive II (Konjunktiv II) for reading.** Forms like *wäre*, *hätte*, *könnte*, *würde* + indirect speech are everywhere in literary German and most discursive prose. Subjunctive I (used for reported speech in news/literature) is rarer but worth recognizing.

4. **Case-marked relative pronouns and embedded clauses.** German loves three-level embedded clauses ending in verb pile-ups. Reading practice with conscious attention to which verb belongs to which clause builds the parsing speed you need for Kafka and Sebald. This is most of what makes Sebald feel "difficult."

5. **Conceptual vocabulary directly.** Weltschmerz, Sehnsucht, Schadenfreude, Vergangenheitsbewältigung, Geworfenheit, Unheimlichkeit, Bildung, Heimat, Angst (in its full philosophical weight, not the borrowed English use). Read about them and what they mean in German thought before encountering them in context — it makes the texts feel less mystified.

6. **Frequency-based vocab acquisition, not textbook progression.** A 5,000-word frequency list will get you further faster than chapter-by-chapter textbook vocab.

## Approach

### Phase 1: Refresh & Push to B2 (0–4 months)
- Goethe-Institut's B1 and B2 vocab lists
- *Aspekte Neu B1+/B2* textbook
- Anki deck: 5,000 most frequent German words
- Easy German YouTube + DW Slow German with German subs
- Daily short newspaper reading: Süddeutsche Zeitung, ZEIT, FAZ

### Phase 2: Native Media (4–10 months)
- Start *Dark* with German subs (the dialogue is unusually clear and slow for a thriller)
- Tatort episodes (Krimi tradition — Munich and Berlin variants are clearest dialect-wise; avoid Bavarian-heavy episodes early)
- Read short Krimi novels: Friedrich Ani, Jakob Arjouni, Volker Kutscher (the *Babylon Berlin* source novels)
- Read graded modern fiction: Bernhard Schlink's *Der Vorleser* is accessible

### Phase 3: Literary Entry (10–18 months)
- Kafka's shorter works — *Die Verwandlung*, *In der Strafkolonie*, *Vor dem Gesetz*. His sentences are long but his vocabulary is plain; the difficulty is conceptual, not lexical.
- Daniel Kehlmann's novels
- Bernhard Schlink's full catalog
- Read with a dual-text edition if needed (German + English facing pages)

### Phase 4: Heavy Literary / Philosophical (18 months+)
- Thomas Mann (*Tonio Kröger* before *Buddenbrooks*; *Doktor Faustus* is monumental)
- W. G. Sebald (*Die Ringe des Saturn*, *Austerlitz*) — his sentences are an experience; this is genuinely transformed by reading in German
- Walter Benjamin's essays
- Adorno if you're feeling masochistic but rewarded
- For your taste specifically: also try E. T. A. Hoffmann (uncanny tradition source material — *Der Sandmann*) and Friedrich Dürrenmatt (Swiss-German, *Der Besuch der alten Dame* is karmic-justice theater)

## Commitment Estimate
- **Refreshing to B1 active**: 6–10 weeks
- **B2 / watching Dark in German with German subs**: 6–10 months
- **Reading Krimi novels comfortably**: 9–15 months
- **Reading Kafka in original**: 12–18 months (faster than people expect — his lexicon is plain)
- **Reading Sebald / Mann fluidly**: 2.5–4 years

For your aim specifically, the genre-TV target is reachable inside a year of consistent work, and the literary target is reachable inside 2–3 years.

## What to Skip or Defer
- Goethe-Institut certification unless needed for work/study
- Bavarian / Swiss / Austrian dialect study — fascinating but not on the critical path
- Conversation practice if your aim is reception
- Writing practice beyond what's needed for vocab retention
- Old orthography / pre-1996 spelling — Kafka edition you'll buy will use modern spelling anyway

## Resources Worth the Money
- **Lingoda** or **Babbel** for refresh structure
- **DWDS** (Digitales Wörterbuch der deutschen Sprache) — best German dictionary online, free
- **DW (Deutsche Welle) Learn German section** — solid free graded content
- **Easy German YouTube channel**
- **Reading**: ZEIT and FAZ for serious journalistic register; Süddeutsche for general; *Der Spiegel* for politics

## Signposts for Progress
- Week 8: Active B1 restored — can follow a podcast at slow speed
- Month 4: Watch a Tatort episode and follow the plot without pausing constantly
- Month 8: Start Dark in German with German subs
- Month 14: Finish *Der Vorleser* unaided
- Month 18: Read Kafka's *Vor dem Gesetz* and feel it
- Month 30: Begin Sebald
