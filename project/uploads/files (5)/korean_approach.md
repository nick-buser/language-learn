# Korean — Approach & Roadmap

## Aim
Access K-drama, webtoons, and Korean film in the original — specifically to capture the relational/honorific layer and the catharsis-loop emotional vocabulary that subtitles systematically flatten. Eventually move into Han Kang, Park Min-gyu, and other contemporary Korean literary fiction.

## Current Level
- Hangul: readable, can sound out
- Grammar: basic particles, conjugation outline (mostly transferred from Japanese)
- Vocabulary: near zero
- Listening / speaking: untrained

## What You Already Have (and Should Lean On Hard)
Coming from N3–N2 Japanese, Korean is one of the most favorable language transfers in existence. Treating it as "starting from scratch" understates your position by roughly a year.

- **Grammar is nearly isomorphic.** SOV order, topic-prominent, particle-marked cases, verb-final, agglutinative endings. You won't have to relearn how a sentence works.
- **Formality stacking** maps onto keigo conceptually, though Korean's system is sharper-edged and more socially active in everyday speech than modern Japanese keigo.
- **Sino-Korean vocabulary (한자어, ~60% of formal Korean)** shares roots with Japanese kanji compounds. 학교 (hakkyo) = 学校. 도서관 (doseogwan) = 図書館. 가능 (ganeung) = 可能. The entire abstract/news/literary vocabulary unlocks via a sound-shift heuristic. This is a 30–50% vocab head start that beginner courses don't tell you about — many curricula teach Sino-Korean words as if they were unrelated to anything.
- **Counters and number system** structurally familiar (native vs Sino numbers parallel kun vs on readings).

## High-Payoff Early Focus

These are the things standard textbook order buries but that produce disproportionate returns for your aim:

1. **The full speech-level system, not just polite/casual.** Hapsyo-che (-습니다), haeyo-che (-아요/어요), hae-che (-아/어), haera-che (-다/-라). Drill the markers and shifts between them — every K-drama power scene is built on someone using the wrong level deliberately. The "moment someone drops banmal" beat is genuinely the engine of K-drama dialogue.

2. **Address terms as a grammatical category.** 오빠/형/누나/언니/선배/후배/아저씨/아주머니/사장님 — when each is used, when dropping them is intimacy, when adding them is distance, when switching them is an event. Most courses treat these as vocabulary; treat them as relational grammar.

3. **Sino-Korean sound-shift heuristic.** Learn the systematic mappings from Japanese ON-readings to Korean Sino readings (the k↔g, t↔d, certain vowel and final-consonant shifts). Each pattern unlocks hundreds of words you've technically already studied. There are good resources for this online; an afternoon of work pays off for years.

4. **Han, jeong, nunchi, hwabyeong as cultural concepts.** Read about them early in English so that when you encounter scenes orchestrated around them — and they will be, constantly — you recognize what the show is doing. Hwabyeong (anger illness from suppressed injustice) is literally a recognized cultural-syndrome diagnosis in Korean psychiatry and the actual etiology of half the protagonists in K-drama.

5. **Native vs Sino number systems and their counters.** This trips people up forever; getting it sorted in week two saves months.

6. **Particle nuance, especially 은/는 vs 이/가.** This is the perpetual stumbling block. Japanese は/が intuition mostly transfers but with edge cases that matter; build a small notebook of cases that surprise you.

## Approach

### Phase 1: Foundation (0–4 months)
- *Korean Grammar in Use Beginning* + Talk To Me In Korean Levels 1–4
- Anki deck: TOPIK I vocabulary (~1,500 words) — accelerate via Sino-Korean transfer
- Listening: K-drama clips with Korean subs (not English), short 1–2 minute scenes repeated several times
- Production: shadow K-drama dialogue for accent (your Japanese phonology already handles ~70% of Korean sounds; the remaining work is mostly aspirated consonants and ㅓ/ㅡ)

### Phase 2: Consolidation (4–10 months)
- *Korean Grammar in Use Intermediate*
- Read children's webtoons (네이버 웹툰 has accessible options); progress to slice-of-life
- Watch a full K-drama with Korean subs — *My Mister* is dense and dialogue-driven; *Reply 1988* is good for natural family register; avoid action-heavy shows where you'd lean on visual
- TOPIK II vocabulary deck

### Phase 3: Media Access (10–18 months)
- Read a full webtoon arc unaided (Bastard, Sweet Home, Pigpen all work — Pigpen has the cleanest prose for learners)
- Watch *Stranger / Forest of Secrets* with Korean subs — legal/political vocabulary will stretch you but the dialogue is precise and rewarding
- Start reading Korean Wikipedia entries in your areas of interest

### Phase 4: Literary (18 months+)
- Han Kang (*The Vegetarian*, *Human Acts*) — accessible prose, profound content
- Park Min-gyu, Bae Suah — more challenging
- Lee Chang-dong screenplays if you can find them

## Commitment Estimate
- **Watching K-drama with Korean subs comfortably**: 10–14 months of consistent work (~45 min/day)
- **Reading webtoons fluently**: 12–18 months
- **Reading literary fiction**: 2–3 years
- **Understanding K-drama without subs**: 2.5–4 years (listening is the long tail)

The Japanese transfer compresses these timelines by maybe 25–35% versus a cold start. Pronunciation work is genuinely easier from Japanese (fewer phonemic ranges to acquire), but Korean has consonant assimilation rules absent in Japanese that take time to internalize for listening.

## What to Skip or Defer
- TOPIK exam prep unless you need the certification — the format doesn't serve your aim
- Conversation classes early — your aim is reception, so prioritize listening and reading over speaking until comprehension is built
- Pop song lyrics as study material (often poetic-fragmentary, not representative of natural speech)

## Resources Worth the Money
- **TTMIK** (free + paid courses, including their grammar series)
- **LingQ** for graded reading
- **Naver dictionary** (free, has example sentences, much better than Google Translate)
- **HowToStudyKorean.com** (free, comprehensive grammar reference)
- **Anki** with a TOPIK-frequency deck

## Signposts for Progress
- Month 3: Read a children's book unaided
- Month 6: Follow a K-drama scene without subs on second viewing
- Month 10: Read a webtoon panel and get the joke before checking translation
- Month 14: Notice a speech-level shift in dialogue and catch what it means
- Month 18: Read a Han Kang short story with dictionary support
