## The Specific Bottleneck
French is in some ways the easiest of these languages for a native English speaker — 30%+ Latinate vocabulary overlap, similar sentence structure, no cases — but it has a specific listening bottleneck: connected speech features (elision, liaison, enchaînement, dropped letters, casual register changes) make French sound nothing like its written form at native speed. Most American learners can read French four or five years before they can understand spoken French.

That listening gap is the specific thing to attack early for your aim. Don't repeat the high-school sequence; attack the gap directly.

## What You Already Have
- High-school foundation is doing more than you think — passive vocabulary recognition typically survives decades of dormancy
- Conjugation patterns probably partially intact for present, passé composé, imparfait
- Latinate cognates with English mean reading recovery is fast
- (Possibly) some Spanish helping with Romance language intuition

## High-Payoff Early Focus

1. **Spoken-vs-written register gap, attacked directly.** Don't just read texts and listen to formal news. Listen to cinematic dialogue, podcasts at native speed, France Inter radio, casual YouTube. The thing that frustrates intermediate learners is that "Qu'est-ce que tu fais?" comes out sounding like "Skəfɛ?" Get used to this fast or you'll plateau.

2. **Subjunctive mood for reading.** French uses the subjunctive constantly in literary and theoretical writing. *Que je sois, que tu aies, qu'il soit, qu'il fasse* — recognize them on sight; produce them later if at all.

3. **Argot and informal register.** Putain, ouais, bof, ça craint, c'est dingue, mec, meuf, pote, kiffer, choper, taffer. French cinema dialogue lives in this register; high-school French does not teach it. Skip the prudishness — this is the actual spoken language.

4. **Tu/vous distinction as social information.** The shift between them in a scene is plot-relevant. You probably know this from high school; pay attention to it actively when watching.

5. **Imperfect vs passé composé vs passé simple.** The passé simple is the literary past tense — you'll meet it in any novel — and learners often defer it indefinitely. Recognize it early so it doesn't trip you up when you open Camus or Modiano.

6. **Reading philosophy directly if relevant.** French philosophical prose has its own register — abstract nouns, parallel constructions, careful subjunctive use, complex hypotaxis. If continental theory is your target, this is what you'll be reading; it's a different reading-skill from novels.

## Approach

### Phase 1: Refresh (0–3 months)
- Anki: French frequency lists (~3,000 most common)
- *Assimil French With Ease* if you want a structured re-entry — old-school but works
- Duolingo for daily habit if it helps consistency
- Listen to French podcasts at slow speed: *News in Slow French*, *InnerFrench*, *Coffee Break French*

### Phase 2: Native Media Push (3–10 months)
- *Coffee Break French* intermediate
- Watch French cinema with French subs — start with talky films:
  - Audiard's *Un Prophète*
  - Sciamma's *Portrait de la jeune fille en feu*
  - Rohmer's *Conte d'été* (slower, clearer)
  - Desplechin's *Comment je me suis disputé...*
- Read graded French fiction or short accessible novels: Modiano (short, accessible), Houellebecq (controversial but accessible)
- French YouTube — Linguisticae, Astronogeek, Cyrus North for pop-intellectual content; HugoDécrypte for news

### Phase 3: Literary / Cinematic (10–24 months)
- Camus (*L'Étranger* is the canonical first French novel for a reason — simple prose, deep content)
- Modiano (any of his short novels)
- Annie Ernaux (recent Nobel, plain prose, devastating)
- French film without English subs: Claire Denis, Carax, Bonello
- Begin theory if interested: Barthes' shorter essays (*Mythologies*), Foucault interviews

### Phase 4: Deep Literary / Theoretical (24+ months)
- Proust if you're committing
- Houellebecq's longer novels
- Continental theory: Foucault, Derrida (depending on appetite)
- New Wave cinema in original (Godard, Truffaut)

## Commitment Estimate
- **Refresh to functional B1**: 3–6 months
- **Watching contemporary French film with French subs comfortably**: 10–16 months
- **Reading modern French novels**: 14–24 months
- **Reading Proust / theoretical French**: 3–5 years
- **Understanding films without subs**: 3–5 years (the listening tail is the long one)

For your specific aim, refresh-to-cinema is the most realistic 12-month target. Going to literature is straightforward from there; theoretical French is a separate, larger project.

## What to Skip or Defer
- DELF/DALF certification unless needed
- Quebec French unless that's your specific target — different listening profile
- Production fluency beyond what's needed for retention
- French grammar perfectionism — French native speakers themselves get half the agreement rules wrong; let it go
- Writing accent marks by hand — irrelevant to your aim

## Honest Note on Priority
You mentioned not having found French media that grabs you yet. This is consistent with the prior conversation's diagnosis: France's intellectual energy went to film and literature, and most French TV is thin compared to its cinema. If you commit to French, commit to it for cinema and literature specifically — those are the genuine returns. If TV is your medium, this is the wrong language to invest in over Korean.

The case for French is strongest if continental theory or art-cinema becomes genuinely interesting to you. Otherwise, it's a "nice to have" rather than a high-priority target.
