# Japanese — Approach & Roadmap

## Aim
Move from comfortable-but-bounded N3/N2 territory into genuine media access: unsubbed anime, light novels, literary manga (Urasawa, Asano, Matsumoto), and eventually Murakami / Abe / Ogawa / Yokomizo in original. Visual novels and JRPGs as a bonus payoff.

## Current Level (Self-Reported)
- Grammar: solid through N3, partial N2
- Speaking / listening / accent: decent
- Vocab: weak relative to grammar
- Kanji: weak relative to grammar

## The Specific Bottleneck
Your profile is the classic "spoken-strong, written-weak" pattern that comes from immersion-heavy or audio-heavy study. Grammar is the hardest part of Japanese to acquire, and you've done it. The remaining work is vocabulary and kanji acquisition at scale plus reading-speed development — and these compound on each other, since vocab gaps slow reading, which slows vocab acquisition.

You're past the wall most people quit at. The compounding from here is favorable. But vocab/kanji acquisition at the scale needed for adult literary content is genuinely a multi-thousand-hour task with no real shortcuts; it's discipline, not insight, that closes the gap.

## What You Already Have
- Grammar intuition that lets you parse complex sentences once vocabulary is known
- Sound-system fluency, so audiobook and podcast consumption is viable for vocab acquisition (most learners can't use audio at this stage)
- Speaking practice that has hardwired sentence patterns — you can leverage this for retention by reading aloud, since you can produce the right rhythm and pitch

## High-Payoff Early Focus

1. **Aggressive kanji acceleration.** Pick a system and grind. WaniKani if you want gamified spaced repetition with mnemonics. RTK (Heisig) if you prefer the front-loaded approach. Or vocab-first Anki via Core 2k/6k. The system matters less than the discipline. ~2,000 kanji is the threshold for adult reading; you're likely at 600–1,000, so closing this gap is the single highest-ROI intervention you can make.

2. **Pronoun system explicitly studied.** You probably know watashi/boku/ore. The full system is a characterization vocabulary. When does a character use jibun? Washi (older men, certain regions, anime "grandpa" register)? Ware-ware (formal/archaic)? Atashi vs atakushi? Sessha (sword-era warrior speech, used ironically in modern fiction)? Once you start noticing pronoun-as-characterization, it retroactively changes how you read everything.

3. **Sentence-final particles in depth.** Beyond yo/ne: kana, kashira, dazo, dawa, dayone, naa, sa, ze, mon, mono, ttsu, jan. Each carries gender, age, register, and emotional information. This is where translated dialogue loses the most.

4. **Keigo for reception, not production.** You don't need to produce keigo fluently for your aim, but you need to recognize it instantly: itadaku/itasu/oru/gozaru/irassharu/ossharu and the standard o-/go- + verb stem + -ni naru / -suru pattern. Business and historical drama dialogue is keigo-saturated.

5. **Furigana / ateji literacy.** Read enough manga to internalize the convention of authors using non-standard kanji for established words to carry double meanings. Fujimoto, Asano, Urasawa, and basically all literary mangaka do this constantly; once you start noticing it, you can read it intentionally.

6. **N1 grammar earlier than feels comfortable.** Most N1 grammar items are recognition-only for literary reading. Brute-force the list rather than waiting until you "feel ready."

## Approach

### Phase 1: Vocabulary / Kanji Acceleration (0–6 months)
- Pick a kanji acquisition system and commit
- Target: solid 1,500 kanji + N2 vocab consolidated
- Read graded readers in parallel — Sou Matome or Shin Kanzen Master N2 series
- Manga that bridges: Yotsubato (easy) → Tsuki ga Kirei or slice-of-life seinen → Yokohama Kaidashi Kikou (gentle, beautiful)
- Switch all your anime watching to Japanese subtitles only

### Phase 2: Reading Push (6–14 months)
- N1 grammar (Shin Kanzen Master N1)
- Read your first full manga unaided. Recommended starting points for your taste:
  - *Pluto* (Urasawa, slightly easier than 20CB)
  - *20th Century Boys* (Urasawa, accessible language, masterpiece)
  - *Dorohedoro* (more slang, lots of fun)
  - *Goodnight Punpun* (Asano, devastating, accessible)
- Watch dialogue-heavy anime with JP subs: *Monster*, *Psycho-Pass*, *Mushishi*, *From the New World*

### Phase 3: Literary Entry (14–24 months)
- *Konbini Ningen* by Murata Sayaka — short, contemporary, accessible
- Murakami short stories before novels — *The Elephant Vanishes*, *Men Without Women*
- Light novels in your registers (Ryōgo Narita, Otaro Maijo)
- Visual novels: *Steins;Gate* is accessible; *Umineko* is hard but worth it

### Phase 4: Deep Literary (24 months+)
- Murakami novels
- Kōbō Abe (*Suna no Onna*)
- Yōko Ogawa
- Yokomizo Seishi for classic mystery prose
- Edogawa Ranpo short stories
- *13 Sentinels: Aegis Rim* in Japanese (the localization is good but the original is sharper)

## Commitment Estimate
- **Reading manga in your taste range fluently**: 8–14 months
- **Watching anime with JP subs comfortably**: 6–12 months (you may be closer than you think)
- **Reading light novels**: 14–20 months
- **Reading literary fiction (Murakami, Abe)**: 2–4 years
- **Untrained ear for unsubbed anime in dialogue-heavy genres**: 1.5–3 years

The grammar foundation you have is doing a lot of work here. From your level, the path to manga-fluency is shorter than from a cold start by maybe 40%.

## What to Skip or Defer
- JLPT prep as an end in itself — N1 is useful as a forcing function for reading, but the test format itself won't help your aim
- Speaking practice beyond what you're already doing — diminishing returns for reception-focused goals
- Studying historical Japanese (bungo) unless you specifically want pre-1900 literature
- Pitch accent perfectionism — useful for speaking, irrelevant for reading
- Pencil-and-paper kanji writing — useful for production, but reading-focused learners can skip it

## Resources Worth the Money
- **WaniKani** (paid, gamified kanji + vocab SRS)
- **BunPro** (grammar SRS, well-tuned to JLPT)
- **Yomitan** browser extension (game-changing for digital reading; was previously Yomichan)
- **Anki** + mining decks if you can handle the setup
- **Satori Reader** (graded reading at intermediate level)
- **Animelon** for anime with toggleable JP subs and dictionary lookup

## Signposts for Progress
- Month 3: Read Yotsubato unaided in one sitting
- Month 6: Watch an episode of a slice-of-life anime with JP subs and not feel exhausted
- Month 12: Finish your first volume of Pluto
- Month 18: Read a full Murakami short story with light dictionary support
- Month 24: Start a Murakami novel without dread
