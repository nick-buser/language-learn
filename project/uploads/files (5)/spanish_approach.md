# Spanish — Approach & Roadmap

## Aim
Bridge the reading-versus-listening gap that's currently leaving you stranded, then push toward Borges, Bolaño, Cortázar, and Schweblin in original — the writers whose work most closely resembles the theory-craft mystery-box experience you've been describing in TV.

## Current Level (Self-Reported)
- Reading: ~Little Prince level, couple thousand passive words
- Listening at natural speed: weak
- Conversation: weak
- Thinking in Spanish: not yet
- Pronunciation: fine

## The Specific Bottleneck
You have the classic asymmetric learner profile: a lot of passive vocabulary, no active retrieval speed, and an untrained ear. This is a common and very fixable situation — the underlying competence is there; it just hasn't been activated for spoken or interactive use.

The fix is concentrated listening and shadowing, not more reading. Continuing to read without working the ear will widen the gap, not close it.

## What You Already Have
- Latinate vocabulary overlap with English (and any French background reinforcing it)
- Reading competence you can leverage for content selection
- Pronunciation already calibrated, which is one of the harder things to fix later
- Passive vocabulary base that just needs activation

## High-Payoff Early Focus

1. **Listening at natural speed, deliberately.** Not slowed-down podcasts. Real native-speed audio — radio, podcasts, film dialogue. Initially it will sound impossible; that's the necessary discomfort. Repeating the same 5-minute audio clip 8–10 times across days dramatically accelerates ear training. This is the single most important intervention for your current state.

2. **Choose a target dialect.** Argentinian (rioplatense) for Borges, Cortázar, Bolaño, Schweblin. Mexican for the largest film and TV catalog. Iberian for Spain's prose and cinema. They diverge in phonology and vocabulary enough to matter for intermediate ear training; pick one to learn first, broaden later.

3. **Subjunctive mood, especially imperfect subjunctive.** Spanish uses it pervasively — counterfactuals, polite requests, expressions of doubt and emotion, constantly in literary writing. Borges's prose lives in it. Recognize *fuera/fuese, hiciera/hiciese, dijera/dijese* on sight; produce them later.

4. **Por vs para, ser vs estar, preterite vs imperfect.** The classic intermediate stumbling blocks. Most Spanish learners are stuck on these for years; isolating them and drilling them past the intuition threshold pays off massively. Buy a dedicated workbook for each and grind through it.

5. **Reading aloud (shadowing).** Take an audiobook chapter, read along with the audio, then read aloud without it. This converts passive vocabulary to active and trains the ear simultaneously. It's the single most effective intervention for your specific gap — more effective than separate listening and reading practice.

6. **Argentine slang and voseo** if you go that route — *vos sos* vs *tú eres*, *che*, *boludo*, *quilombo*, etc. Borges and Cortázar themselves write fairly standard literary Spanish but use Argentine forms; modern Argentine cinema (Trapero, Mitre, Llinás) is full of voseo.

## Approach

### Phase 1: Activation (0–4 months)
- Drop reading-only practice for now; you have enough passive reading
- Daily listening: 30–45 min of native-speed Spanish, repeated clips
- Shadowing: 10–15 min daily
- *Dreaming Spanish* YouTube channel — superb comprehensible-input source
- iTalki tutors for conversation practice — 2–3 sessions per week for 3 months will close the active-production gap faster than anything else (highest ROI single intervention available)

### Phase 2: Comprehension Build (4–10 months)
- Continue listening expansion
- Watch Spanish-language film and TV with Spanish subs — try Argentine cinema if Borges/Bolaño is the target: *Relatos Salvajes* is accessible and excellent; *El Secreto de sus Ojos*; Lucrecia Martel's *La Ciénaga* (you've seen it, but in Spanish with Spanish subs is a different experience)
- Read short stories with audio: Cortázar's *Bestiario* is short, supernatural-uncanny, and audio readings exist online

### Phase 3: Literary Entry (10–18 months)
- Borges' *Ficciones* — short, but dense; read slowly. This is probably the highest-value literary target for your taste profile.
- Cortázar's short stories — *Casa Tomada*, *Continuidad de los parques*, *Axolotl*
- Bolaño's short stories before *2666* — *Llamadas Telefónicas*, *Putas Asesinas*
- Modern accessible: Mariana Enriquez's *Las cosas que perdimos en el fuego* (Argentine literary horror — very your register)
- Samanta Schweblin's *Distancia de Rescate* (short, dread-saturated)

### Phase 4: Deep Literary (18 months+)
- Bolaño *2666*, *Los Detectives Salvajes*
- García Márquez (different register — magical realism — less your taste, but cultural foundation)
- Onetti, Vila-Matas, Piglia
- Latin American cinema unsubbed: Argentine, Chilean, Mexican

## Commitment Estimate
- **Bridging the listening gap to functional comprehension**: 6–10 months (faster than people think, once focused on listening directly)
- **Reading Borges and Cortázar short stories**: ~12 months from current level
- **Reading Bolaño's longer novels comfortably**: 18–30 months
- **Watching Argentine cinema without subs**: 2–3 years

For your stated aim — the literary tradition rather than the streaming output — this is probably the language where investment-to-target distance is shortest among the languages where you don't already have strong foundation. The literary target (Borges in original) is realistically reachable inside 12 months with focused effort.

## What to Skip or Defer
- DELE certification unless needed
- More reading-only practice — you've maxed out the value of that for now
- Spanish-Spanish vs Latin American debates — pick one, move on
- Writing practice — not on the critical path for reception aims
- Trying to memorize all six subjunctive tenses at once — focus on imperfect subjunctive first

## Signposts for Progress
- Month 2: Can follow a Dreaming Spanish intermediate video at normal speed
- Month 5: Can hold a halting 30-minute conversation with a tutor
- Month 8: Watch *Relatos Salvajes* and follow it with Spanish subs
- Month 12: Read a Borges short story unaided
- Month 18: Read Schweblin's *Distancia de Rescate* without dictionary
- Month 24: Start *2666*

## Honest Note on Priority
Spanish has an unusual profile in your stack: the TV pipeline doesn't match your taste, but the literary tradition matches it precisely. If you treat Spanish as a literature-access language rather than a TV-access language, the ROI is high. The case for it is essentially: Borges and Bolaño are exactly the writers your taste profile points to, and they're underserved by translation in specific ways (Borges's Latinate sentence rhythm, Bolaño's voice-shifting cadence). Worth investing in if you genuinely want to read those writers; less compelling otherwise.
