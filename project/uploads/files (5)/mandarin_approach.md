# Mandarin — Approach & Roadmap

## Aim
Build to a level that allows you to read Liu Cixin in original, eventually Yu Hua and other contemporary literary fiction, plus Chinese-language film with Chinese subs. This is the longest-haul language of the set.

## Current Level (Self-Reported)
- Tones: basic awareness
- Vocabulary: ~100–200 words
- Characters: ~100 most frequent
- Listening / reading / speaking: minimal

## The Specific Bottleneck
Mandarin has the steepest learning curve among major languages for native English speakers (FSI Category IV — same as Japanese and Korean, but with arguably higher character-system load). The bottleneck splits into:

- **Tones in connected speech** — not isolated drilling. Tone sandhi rules, especially 3rd-tone interactions
- **Character acquisition at scale** — 3,000+ characters needed for adult literacy
- **Listening comprehension** — the high frequency of homophones disambiguated by tone makes listening genuinely harder than Japanese or Korean

The compensating good news: grammar is comparatively easy. No conjugation, no cases, no plurals, no tense morphology. The work is almost entirely vocabulary, characters, tones, and listening training.

## What You Already Have
- Tone awareness — you've already done the conceptual lift
- A starter set of high-frequency characters
- Modest Sino-Japanese transfer, though be careful: sound shifts from Japanese ON-readings to Mandarin are looser than Japanese-to-Korean shifts. Use it as a hint, not a reliable predictor.

## High-Payoff Early Focus

1. **Tone pairs over isolated tones.** Drill all 16 (or 20 with neutral) tone-pair combinations as units, not individual tones. Real speech is in pairs and triples; isolated-tone drilling produces speakers who can't be understood at speed and listeners who can't follow native speech. The Sinosplice "Tone Pairs" series is the canonical resource.

2. **Tone sandhi rules** explicitly: third-tone reduction (3+3 → 2+3), the special behavior of 不 and 一. Don't defer past 6 months.

3. **Aspect markers vs tense.** Chinese uses 了 (le — completion / change of state), 过 (guò — experiential past), 着 (zhe — continuous / durative) instead of tense conjugation. Get the conceptual distinction early; this is where most learners fight English-language intuition for years. 了 in particular is the most overloaded word in the language.

4. **Measure words (量词).** Yes, drill them. 一个人, 一本书, 一只猫, 一张纸 — each noun pairs with specific measure words. Mandarin without them sounds babyish. ~50 cover most needs.

5. **Top 1,500 characters by frequency** rather than HSK order. The top 1,500 cover ~95% of running text. Use a frequency-based Anki deck for faster reading payoff. HSK order is fine for testing, slow for reading capability.

6. **Chengyu (四字成语) — 4-character idioms.** Start collecting from the start, even just ~50 common ones. They're frequent in literary Chinese, news, and dramatic dialogue, and unlike most vocabulary they're effectively unguessable from components. They're also genuinely fun — each one is a compressed story.

7. **Jianghu and wuxia conceptual vocabulary** if you want to access the martial-arts tradition (which shapes a huge amount of Chinese genre fiction): 江湖 (jianghu), 侠 (xia), 恩 (en), 仇 (chou), 义 (yi), 武林 (wulin). These structure both classical and modern Chinese genre fiction.

8. **Simplified vs Traditional decision, made early.** Mainland and Singapore use simplified; Taiwan and Hong Kong use traditional. Pick based on your target media. Liu Cixin originals are simplified; Wong Kar-wai's HK and Taiwan film subs are traditional. You can learn both later but learn one well first.

## Approach

### Phase 1: Foundation (0–6 months)
- Pinyin and tone drilling — Sinosplice tone pairs, ChinesePod elementary
- *Integrated Chinese* Volume 1, or HSK 1–2 textbooks
- Anki: top 1,500 characters by frequency + base vocabulary
- 30 min/day of listening: Slow Chinese, ChinesePod, Mandarin Corner

### Phase 2: Consolidation (6–14 months)
- HSK 3–4 vocabulary; *Integrated Chinese* Volume 2–3
- Mandarin Companion graded readers (Sherlock Holmes adapted at 300-word, 450-word, 1,200-word levels — excellent stepping stones)
- Watch simple Chinese TV: dubbed children's content or slow modern dramas with Chinese subs
- Read Chinese internet content (Weibo, Zhihu)

### Phase 3: Native Entry (14–28 months)
- Push to ~3,000 characters
- Read your first novel: probably a contemporary YA or genre novel rather than literary
- Watch Chinese drama with Chinese subs (the dialogue is generally clearer than Japanese or Korean equivalents at this stage)
- Start sampling Chinese sci-fi short stories — Han Song, Xia Jia

### Phase 4: Literary / Cinematic (28 months+)
- Liu Cixin trilogy (*Three Body Problem* / 《三体》 — denser in original than the translation suggests)
- Yu Hua (*To Live* / 《活着》, *Chronicle of a Blood Merchant* / 《许三观卖血记》) — plainer prose, devastating content
- Lu Xun's short stories — landmark texts in modern Chinese fiction
- Wong Kar-wai films with Chinese subs (note: most HK films are originally Cantonese, but subs and a significant portion of dialogue are Mandarin-accessible)

## Commitment Estimate
- **Conversational basics**: 6–10 months
- **Watching contemporary Chinese drama with Chinese subs**: 18–28 months
- **Reading contemporary genre fiction**: 2.5–4 years
- **Reading Liu Cixin in original**: 3–5 years
- **Reading Yu Hua / Lu Xun comfortably**: 3.5–6 years

Mandarin is the language where you should be most realistic about the time investment. There are no shortcuts; the character system means the curve is steep and long. If you have a fixed time budget across multiple languages, this should probably be the lowest priority unless Chinese literature/cinema is a specific high-value target for you.

## Cantonese Note
If your interest is HK cinema (Wong Kar-wai, Johnnie To, Infernal Affairs), Mandarin won't fully unlock it. Cantonese is a separate language by linguistics-standard measures despite sharing the writing system. Learning Mandarin to read subtitles + Cantonese for listening is one strategy if you really care about that cinema. Realistically, this is a separate ~1,500-hour project on top of Mandarin.

## What to Skip or Defer
- Traditional characters unless your target media require them
- Classical Chinese (文言文) — irrelevant to modern media; fascinating but a separate, larger project
- Calligraphy as a study method — doesn't help reading at scale
- HSK certification unless required for work/study
- Producing tones perfectly — recognition matters more than production for reception goals

## Resources Worth the Money
- **The Chairman's Bao** (graded Chinese reading, paid but excellent)
- **Pleco dictionary** (free, gold standard, paid add-ons worth it)
- **Skritter** (character writing practice if you decide writing is needed)
- **Mandarin Companion** graded readers
- **YouTube**: ChinesePod, Mandarin Corner, Comprehensible Mandarin, Lazy Chinese
- **Du Chinese** app for graded reading with audio

## Signposts for Progress
- Month 3: Hold a basic conversation about food, family, work
- Month 9: Read a Mandarin Companion 450-word reader unaided
- Month 18: Follow a slow Chinese drama scene with Chinese subs
- Month 30: Read a Yu Hua short story with heavy dictionary support
- Month 48: Start *The Three Body Problem* in Chinese

## Honest Recommendation
Given your taste profile, your time, and your existing investments, Mandarin is likely the lowest-priority language to pursue actively unless Chinese sci-fi or Yu Hua specifically become important targets. The Korean transfer from Japanese is enormous; the German refresh is fast; Mandarin from where you are is years of grinding before payoff. Worth pursuing if it's a genuine long-term interest, but worth honestly assessing the opportunity cost first.
